The Reality

What Breaks First
in Distribution Technology

Zero Secondary Sales Visibility
You know how much you shipped to distributors. You have no reliable data on how much they sold to retailers. By the time secondary sales data reaches you, it's a week old and partially inaccurate.
Scheme Reconciliation at Month-End
Trade schemes are configured in spreadsheets, communicated via WhatsApp, and reconciled weeks after the fact. Distributor claims don't match internal records. Finance spends days resolving discrepancies.
Beat Planning Without Data
Field reps plan their own routes without system support. High-potential outlets get visited weekly. Low-coverage areas go unvisited for months. You find out when a competitor wins the shelf.
Distributor Credit Is a Spreadsheet
Outstanding balances and credit limits for hundreds of distributors are managed manually. Orders get booked against distributors who are already over limit. Credit risk is discovered at collection, not at order booking.
No Distributor Stock Visibility
You don't know how much inventory a distributor is holding until they place the next replenishment order. Overstocked distributors sit on working capital. Understocked ones lose sales you never see.
SFA App That Nobody Uses
The salesperson app was built or bought, rolled out to the field, and adoption collapsed within months. Reps fall back to WhatsApp orders and manual reporting. The app data doesn't reflect reality.
DMS and ERP Are Out of Sync
Primary orders booked in the DMS don't reflect in the ERP until end-of-day batch processing. Finance is reconciling on yesterday's data. Dispatch decisions happen without visibility into actual confirmed orders.
Returns and Claims Are a Black Hole
Distributor returns - expired stock, damaged goods, scheme claims - are logged differently across regions. Some go into the ERP, some don't. Reconciliation against what was actually returned takes weeks.
Market Coverage Is Unknown
You know your revenue by geography. You don't know which retailers are being actively covered, which have dropped off, or where coverage gaps are letting a competitor build shelf presence you don't know about.
Seasonal Schemes Operationally Painful
Festival season schemes require configuring offers across hundreds of distributors and thousands of SKUs. It takes a week to deploy, runs on static spreadsheets, and creates chaos when the field doesn't get the right information in time.

DMS Architecture Flow

How a Well-Architected DMS
Runs Distribution End to End

The gap in most distribution operations isn't primary sales - it's everything downstream. Primary orders are in the ERP. Secondary sales are on WhatsApp. Distributor stock is unknown. A well-designed DMS closes each of those gaps systematically, layer by layer.

01
Order Booking via SFA App
Field reps book retailer orders through the salesperson app at the point of visit. The app shows real-time scheme eligibility, distributor stock availability, and the retailer's order history. Offline-first design ensures orders are captured even without network connectivity and synced when the connection returns. The app is the first data collection point - if adoption fails here, the entire downstream visibility breaks.
02
Scheme Validation at Order Time
As each order is booked, the scheme engine validates eligibility against the current rules - quantity thresholds, SKU combinations, distributor segment, geography, and scheme period. Applicable discounts and freebies are calculated and attached to the order record. This eliminates the month-end scheme claim reconciliation problem: the scheme is locked at booking, not negotiated after the fact.
03
Distributor Fulfilment & Secondary Sales Capture
Approved orders are dispatched by the distributor and invoiced to the retailer. The DMS captures this as a secondary sales event - distributor-to-retailer - updating the distributor's stock balance and creating a secondary sales record. This is the most critical data point in distribution: it's what feeds accurate demand signals into your planning systems, not primary orders placed in batch.
04
Primary Order Generation & ERP Sync
When distributor stock falls below reorder level, the DMS generates a primary replenishment order from company to distributor. This is synced to the ERP in real-time, not as a batch upload. The ERP confirms dispatch, which updates the distributor's incoming stock position in the DMS. Primary and secondary flows are connected through this loop rather than operating in parallel silos.
05
Credit & Outstanding Balance Enforcement
Each order booking checks the distributor's current outstanding balance against their credit limit before confirmation. If the distributor is over limit or has overdue invoices beyond the configured threshold, the order is held for credit approval. This happens at booking, not at dispatch - preventing fulfilled orders that become collection problems. Credit data is synced from ERP in near-real-time, not from a nightly batch.
06
Analytics: Coverage, Performance & Demand Signals
Secondary sales data, beat adherence records, and scheme redemption data feed into the analytics layer. Coverage analytics shows which outlets were visited vs planned, which haven't been visited in X days, and where coverage is deteriorating. Demand signals from secondary sales flow into demand planning for more accurate replenishment. Scheme ROI is calculated against actual redemption, not estimated reach.

Custom-Built DMS
Custom SFADistributor PortalERP-Integrated
Custom DMS platforms are common when the business has unusual channel structures, specific scheme complexity, or ERP integrations that off-the-shelf platforms can't accommodate. The typical failure mode is a custom DMS that works well at 50 distributors and breaks at 500. Advisory covers identifying where the architecture is hitting its ceiling and designing the targeted re-architecture that unblocks scale without a full rebuild.
Scheme Management Engine
Rules EngineStackable OffersBudget CapsEligibility Logic
Scheme management is the highest-complexity module in any DMS. Trade schemes combine quantity conditions, SKU eligibility, distributor segment rules, geography, stacking rules, and budget caps - all of which change every month. A rules-based scheme engine configured by marketing without engineering involvement, with scheme validation at order booking rather than month-end, is the architecture standard. Advisory covers both the engine design and the operational workflow for scheme configuration and reconciliation.
Beat Planning & Outlet Master
Outlet MasterRoute SchedulingVisit ComplianceCoverage Analytics
A beat planning system starts with a clean outlet master - every retail outlet geocoded, classified by channel, size, and segment. Beats are routes assigned to field reps with scheduled visit frequency by outlet tier. The system tracks planned vs actual visits, identifies coverage gaps, and surfaces declining coverage before it becomes a sales problem. Advisory covers outlet master design, beat construction methodology, and the analytics layer that makes coverage actionable.
ERP Integration Layer
SAP IntegrationReal-Time SyncOrder ConfirmationCredit Enforcement
DMS-ERP integration is where most distribution systems leak data. The integration must cover: primary order sync in real-time (not end-of-day batch), dispatch confirmation back to DMS, credit limit and outstanding balance sync, scheme deduction posting, and returns and claims reconciliation. Error handling - what happens when the ERP is down or rejects an order - needs to be designed explicitly, not discovered in production when a batch fails overnight.
Modernization

Transforming Distribution Operations
Without Disrupting the Channel

Distribution channel disruption is expensive. Distributors resist system changes. Field reps resist new apps. Re-onboarding hundreds of distributors to a new portal carries real business risk during the transition.

The modernization approach I use is additive and phased. Start with order booking and scheme management - the highest-pain, highest-ROI modules. Validate adoption and data quality. Then extend to secondary sales capture and analytics. Distributor portal last.

The goal is a distribution operation where secondary sales data is as reliable as primary, scheme reconciliation is automated, and field coverage is visible to management in real time - not reconstructed from field reports.

  • Secondary sales visible within 24 hours, not a week late
  • Scheme eligibility validated at order booking, not month-end
  • Distributor credit enforced automatically at order time
  • Primary orders in ERP within minutes, not a nightly batch
  • Beat coverage visible to RSMs and NSMs in real time
  • SFA app adoption above 80% within 3 months of rollout
  • Demand planning grounded in real secondary sell-through data

FAQ

Common Questions

What types of businesses benefit from DMS advisory?
FMCG companies managing hundreds of distributors, pharma distribution businesses, consumer electronics and FMCD companies with dealer networks, and platforms building DMS products. The common denominator is a multi-tier distribution structure where primary and secondary sales data need to flow back into planning and operations.
Can you help with Bizom or FieldAssist integration with our ERP?
Yes. Both Bizom and FieldAssist have API layers that can be integrated with SAP, Oracle, Tally, and custom ERPs. The integration covers primary order sync, scheme validation, distributor inventory, and secondary sales data. The architecture decisions - sync frequency, data contracts, error handling - are where advisory is most useful.
We have a custom-built DMS that doesn't scale. Where do we start?
The first step is understanding what's actually breaking. Custom DMS systems most commonly fail at scheme management complexity, concurrent order booking under load, and ERP sync reliability. An architecture review identifies which is the binding constraint before any rebuild work starts.
How do we get real-time secondary sales visibility from distributors?
Secondary sales visibility requires either the distributor running your DMS module, uploading a standardised data file, or using a salesperson app that captures retailer billing data at the point of order. Each approach has different adoption rates and data quality trade-offs. The architecture should handle all three fallback modes, not just the ideal scenario.
Our SFA app has very low adoption. Is that a product problem or an architecture problem?
Usually both. Architecturally: if the app requires connectivity in areas with poor network coverage, requires too many steps per order, or doesn't give reps useful data in return (scheme eligibility, stock availability, pending payments), adoption suffers. Offline-first design and reducing the booking flow to the minimum viable steps are the starting points.
What's the right architecture for scheme management in a DMS?
Scheme management needs a rules engine, not hardcoded logic. Schemes have start/end dates, eligibility conditions (distributor tier, geography, SKU, quantity), stacking rules, and budget caps. A rules engine lets marketing configure schemes without engineering involvement and validates eligibility at order booking, not at month-end reconciliation.
How long does a DMS overhaul typically take?
A phased DMS overhaul - starting with order booking and scheme management, then secondary sales capture, then analytics - typically runs 12-20 weeks for the core platform. ERP integration adds 4-8 weeks depending on the ERP. The bottleneck is usually distributor onboarding and field rep training, not the technology build.
